60. Good World-Building Needs Peacocking 🔗

November 25, 2020
In which I argue that great genre fiction centers an unnecessarily overbuilt 'non-basic element' ‚Äî Tolkien's languages, Culture ship names, Hitchhiker's Guide entries ‚Äî that functions as the id of the world, a costly signal of authorial abundance that invites fan participation and spawns fanpedias.
🔗
Plot and character are in some ways the commodity elements of fiction, and how-to books spend 90% of their words on those. But good genre fiction usually seems to center a non-basic element:

LOTR: fake languages
Culture: names of ships
Star Trek: species

This seems important.
🔗
Plot and character are like a platform, but the centered non-basic element (non-basic as in stories can work without them) is the killer app. You don‚Äôt need any specific non-basic thing (few works develop fake languages as deeply as LOTR), but you do need some non-basic things.
🔗
More examples:

- Harry Potter: ‚Äúmagic physics‚Äù (laws governing magical objects/beings)

- Discworld: political science opinions

- Hitchhikers guide: absurdist fragments of history/geography (which is why the Guide is a good prop... a cheap device to hold the fragments)
🔗
- Asimov: Laws (the 3 laws, which segue into the laws of psychohistory)

- Le Guin: temporalities

- PKD: simulations?

Note these are not ‚Äúthemes‚Äù but ontological first class citizens
🔗
I‚Äôm looking for non-singleton classes. The improbability drive is a key plot element but the only one of its class. Whereas for eg Harry Potter has dozens of magical objects/beings with laws. tweet[1]
🔗
Another way to think about it is: non-basic elements provide a ‚Äúthird way‚Äù to start a story:

1. Work out the plot as an outline of beats (plot-driven)

2. Work out the characters as a set of biographical backstories (char driven)

3. Flesh out non-basic element (NBE-driven)
🔗
If you wanted to write an HHG pastiche, arguably you should start with a bunch of encyclopedia-type entries

Asimov pastiche: make up your own laws for something

Culture pastiche: make up a class of things that can be given interesting names
🔗
I agree these are part of world-building but a subset and importantly, overdeveloped beyond would-building needs. For eg every world needs an ontology and a naming scheme for things, but strategically centering them has an effect beyond just world building. tweet[2]
🔗
Perhaps we should call them world-animations. Subsets of world building that, if adequately overdeveloped, turn into engines of plot and character. Ship names are a life-force in Culture but in most books names just ‚Äúdo the job‚Äù they have to in world-building and nothing more.
🔗
Like J. K. Rowling has fun names too, many of which are sly word play allusions (Durmstrang = Sturm und Drang = character of the school) but they don‚Äôt animate the world the way ship names in Culture do. A mere list of ship names evokes a powerful live world for Culture.
🔗
In Freudian terms, I‚Äôm thinking these core non-basic world-animators are like the id of the world, and worldbuilding is more like superego.

The plot and character are the ego.
🔗
I suspect working the id is the most fun part of the writing for the author. Making up languages was the fun for Tolkien. Making up absurd Guide entries was the fun for Adams.

Why: you can tell they‚Äôve overdone it relative to supporting plot/character or portraying the world.
🔗
The ‚Äúbrand‚Äù of the work in a marketing sense flows from this id element but cannot be cynically engineered. It has to come from an animating place of genuine fun-having. Authenticity if you will. Proof-of-fun. Costly signal that the author was in an abundant/extravagant mode.
🔗
Genre fiction at least (if not literary) I think has to flow from a place of abundance for it to manifest escapist potential that sucks you in. This abundance is costly-signaled by essentially peacocking an element of the world-building to extremes.
🔗
